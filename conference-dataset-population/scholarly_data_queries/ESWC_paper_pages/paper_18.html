Paper 18 (Research track)

14. February 20182. May 2018 Silvio Peroni Paper

Schema-agnostic Blocking for Entity Resolution over Noisy Heterogeneous Data

Author(s): Tiago Brasileiro Araújo, Carlos Eduardo Pires, Demetrio Gomes Mestre, Thiago Pereira Nóbrega, Dimas Cassimiro Nascimento, Kostas Stefanidis

Full text: preprint

Abstract: The increasing use of Web systems (e.g., digital libraries, social networks, and e-commerce) has become a valuable source of semi-structured data. In this context, the Entity Resolution task emerges as a fundamental step to integrate multiple knowledge bases or identify similarities between the data (i.e., entities). Usually, blocking techniques are widely applied as the initial step of Entity Resolution approaches in order to avoid computing the comparisons between all pairs of entities (quadratic cost). In practice, heterogeneous and noisy data increase the difficulties faced by blocking techniques. To address these challenges, we proposed the NA-BLOCKER technique, which is capable of tolerating noisy data to extract information regarding the schema and generate high-quality blocks (i.e., blocks that contain only entities with high chances of being considered correspondents). To this end, the NA-BLOCKER applies Locality Sensitive Hashing in order to hash the attribute values of the entities and enable the generation of high-quality blocks, even with the presence of noise in the attribute values. In this sense, since the Semantic Web approaches usually deal with noisy and heterogeneous data, the NA-BLOCKER technique can be useful for these approaches. Based on the experimental results, we can highlight that the NA-BLOCKER presents better results regarding effectiveness than the state-of-the-art technique, namely, BLAST.

Keywords: Entity resolution; Heterogeneous data; Noisy data; Metablocking; Web data

Decision: reject

Review 1 (by Paul Groth)

(RELEVANCE TO ESWC) Entity resolution (linking of data) is a core question for the semantic web community although I wish there was more contextualization with respect to existing semantic web research on ER.
(NOVELTY OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) The algorithm is an extension of existing algorithms to deal with noise
(CORRECTNESS AND COMPLETENESS OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) The approach is well described.
(EVALUATION OF THE STATE-OF-THE-ART) Provides a good overview of traditional entity resolution literature.
(DEMONSTRATION AND DISCUSSION OF THE PROPERTIES OF THE PROPOSED APPROACH) The evaluation against the state of the art is well described but could have been better presented in places.
(REPRODUCIBILITY AND GENERALITY OF THE EXPERIMENTAL STUDY) The code is available - at least here was a url for it. It uses a preexisting set of entity resolution datasets. It is only tested against one state of the art algorithm.
(OVERALL SCORE) This paper describes a blocking algorithm for Entity Resolution to deal with noise data. The proposed blocking algorithm performs better in terms of state of the art (BLAST-2016) on dealing with noisy data but not on execution time. 
Overall, the paper describes a tailored contribution and is well written. I really liked the introduction, which provided several good contextual references. The algorithm is described well, however, it would have been great to provide a pseudo-code version of the algorithm and not just the given figure. This would have helped piece together the notation introduced in the paper. 
I also think the results would have been better presented as a table. It's hard to compare the results of the two different algorithms using the bar chart. Also the mapping between the figure labels (e.g. (a)) and the actual dataset name makes it difficult to interpret. This is especially the case when the values are small (i.e. when there's a lot of noise that's been introduced).
My major criticism of the paper is that I wish it did a larger job contextualizing the approach in the recent literature on entity resolution stemming from the semantic web community; for example: using ensembles of blocking schemes as in [1]; looking at the graph structure inherent in knowledge graphs to perform entity resolution as in [2] or the use of embeddings as nicely summarized in the related work of [3]. Also, there was no mention of the Ontology Alignment Evaluation Initiative (oaei.ontologymatching.org) which has really driven the area of instance matching. I would have expected at least a discussion of why the instance matching data from that evaluation campaign was not used as well. 
Also with respect to the semantic web, it's interesting that the paper dismisses the use of external information during blocking (e.g. dictionaries) because I think the use of background knowledge is a core part of the consideration traditionally made in the SemWeb literature. 
Beyond this criticism, I had a couple of questions that I wish the paper would clarify:
1) the paper states that "the development of noise-aware (or noise-tolerant) approaches is considered as an open research area by several works [13, 8].", however, the cited sources are ~3 years old.. I wonder if there are updated works that verify this. 
2) Practically, setting threshold values can impact the performance of ER algorithms. The paper states that a threshold value of 0.35 based on the work of the BLAST algorithm. I wonder if if more could be said about the parameter sensitivity of both tested algorithms. 
3) With regard to the statical significance of the results, it would be good to clarify what distributions were actually compared with the t-test?
One recommendation for future work would be the use of word or graph embeddings to compute similarity on attribute values instead of just LSH. 
In summary, a well written tight contribution in the space of entity resolution that could do a bit better job of contextualizing in the semantic web literature. 
Comments after rebuttal: Thanks for answering my questions. I'm not changing my evaluation as it hinges much more on the narrow nature of the contribution.

Review 2 (by Khalid Belhajjame)

(RELEVANCE TO ESWC) The solution proposed by the authors does not seem to be specific to semantic web or linked data. The  examples used by the authors throughout the paper are more in line with classicla database problems.
(NOVELTY OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) There are previous proposals that have examined the use of LSH for improving the results of entity resolution processes, and which have not been examined by the authors. Below is some of them:
H. Kim and D. Lee. HARRA: fast iterative hashed record linkage for large-scale
data collections. In EDBT, pages 525–536, 2010.
Wang Q, Cui M, Liang H. Semantic-aware blocking for entity resolution. IEEE Transactions on Knowledge and Data Engineering. 2016 Jan 1;28(1):166-80.
The author should justify why there work is different/better than these work, given that they are using the same technique to address the same problem, namely blocking for improving ER.
(CORRECTNESS AND COMPLETENESS OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) The solution proposed by the authors is clear and sound. The steps used for identifying blocks of entities are clearly identified and presented.
(EVALUATION OF THE STATE-OF-THE-ART) See my comments on the novelty, which tackle this point.
(DEMONSTRATION AND DISCUSSION OF THE PROPERTIES OF THE PROPOSED APPROACH) The solution proposed by the authors has been validated using 5 datasets. Specifically, the authors compared the performance of their method in terms of effectiveness and efficiency to BLAST (an instance-based method that is schema agnostic).
Here, the authors should have also tried in my opinion, to compare the performance of their method to a solution (or solutions) that exploits information about the schema. This can support the claims made by the authors earlier about the ineffectiveness of these methods when dealing with noisy data.
(REPRODUCIBILITY AND GENERALITY OF THE EXPERIMENTAL STUDY) The authors provide a link of the tool on bitbucket

According to the authors, the data used is available on the project link, but I could not find the link.
(OVERALL SCORE) The authors propose a technique based on LSH to improve the blocking of noisy data prior to performing entity resolution.
SP1. The method proposed by the authors is sound and clearly presented.
SP2. The authors report on an empirical evaluation by comparing the effectiveness and efficiency of their approach to the BLAST method
WP1. The use of LSH is not new, there are existing solutions in the lierature that have not been examined by the authors in the related work.
WP2. Reading the paper, I wondereed by the paper was submitted to a semantic web conference on linked data. The authors did not use example of semantic web datasets (RDF) for example. The approach proposed seem to be more relevant to a database oriented conference

Review 4 (by anonymous reviewer)

(RELEVANCE TO ESWC) NA-BLOCKER is presented as a blocking technique. Blocking is usually an initial step to perform entity resolution and entity resolution is an important research topic in the semantic web community.
(NOVELTY OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) This technique is comparable to BLAST, which is a state-of-the-art meta-blocking approach. They both follow a three step pipeline: 1.Schema extraction 2.Blocking 3. Meta-Blocking (pruning). This method showed novelty in the second step, that instead of exploiting Token Blocking which is used in BLAST, it is using the LSH algorithm. This allows NA-BLOCKER to process noisy data in comparison to BLAST and gives better results in terms of Pair completeness, Pair Quality and F-Measure when the data has noise. But this enhancement was costly in terms of execution time. The approach is using the WNP algorithm in the pruning step which has produced the best pruning results [6].
(CORRECTNESS AND COMPLETENESS OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) The general explanation in Section 2 gives enough information on the complete workflow of blocking. It correctly explains the effect of LSH in generation of blocks leads to noise toleration. It correctly shows how applying LSH on blocking gives a better result that token based blocking on noisy data with an example.
(EVALUATION OF THE STATE-OF-THE-ART) I expected a more comprehensive comparison to BLAST. In the section “Related Work” the only mentioned similarity to BLAST is that both BLAST and NA-Blocker adopted LSH. But from my observation both approaches use the same pipeline, the same method of schema extraction, attribute-match induction and the same method of pruning, which is not mentioned directly in this article. The only difference mentioned is that NA-Blocker is using LSH in block generation. It is mentioned “NA-Blocker is using a novel schema-agnostic blocking”, but it is not mentioned that in BLAST the blocking step is using the traditional token based blocking.
In the Section 2 authors have mentioned “To extract loose-schema information, the attribute-match induction technique is applied [20]” . Based on this reference number, which is referring to the BLAST paper, I could guess that “Schema Information Extraction” step of the pipeline should be similar to BLAST. No comparison is provided in the paper. I can state the same objection for the pruning phase. A comparison to token based blocking  is mentioned in the Section 4  but it is not mentioned that BLAST is using token based blocking. To me the overall pipeline of this approach and the “Schema Information Extraction” step and the “Pruning” step “look” very similar to BLAST. I can not state that confidently, because while the paper mentions similar sub-solutions that are used in BLAST, it does not directly mentioning BLAST. 
The  “Related Work” to me is very thin and other state of the art meta-blocking techniques like TYPiMatch [4] and Limes [5]  could be compared.
(DEMONSTRATION AND DISCUSSION OF THE PROPERTIES OF THE PROPOSED APPROACH) Certain terms and notations were not explained (LSH, attribute-match induction, generation of noisy data). Moreover, quite often techniques were not introduced but just named (sometimes not even referenced). For example, from Section 2, I expected the Section “Schema Information Extraction” to explain the algorithm used to perform Attribute-Match Induction. It was not only explained in this Section, it was not even mentioned. Overall examples have been helpful. Some of the given examples were not comprehensible to me, because certain aspects were not explained. Token based blocking is mentioned in section 4 and is used in an example without explaining what is Token based blocking and how it works. It does not explain that this method is not good to apply on a noise free dataset. Because for such a dataset BLAST gives a better result while a execution time that is approximately half of NA-BLOCKER .
(REPRODUCIBILITY AND GENERALITY OF THE EXPERIMENTAL STUDY) The implementation is not reproducible because the provided link to the source code (https://bitbucket.org/tbrasileiro/na-blocker) returns “access denied error”. The experiment procedure and results also are not available online, therefore the evaluation section is not reproducible. The paper pointed to the dataset of BLAST as the resource, but the noisy evaluation data set is different from this.  
The experiment covered 4 of 5 datasets that are used in the BLAST dataset and covered all expected comparisons. (Execution time, Pair completeness, Pair Quality and F-Measur
(OVERALL SCORE) Summary of the Paper:
This technical article presents NA-BLOCKER . A method of blocking data that can be used in tasks that require the comparison of all the the entities. The use of blocking usually speeds up the comparison task by reducing the number of comparisons. Blocking is widely used in entity resolution and therefore, this paper is very central to the semantic web community.  This work tries to handle two types of noise in data: typographical errors and misspelling. The authors have compared this technique with BLAST[1] in terms of Pair Completeness, Pair Quality, F-Measure and execution time. The tolerance of noise is not considered in BLAST and this method is expected to give more significant results by increase of noise in data.
Short description of the problem tackled in the paper, main contributions, and results:
This paper tackles the problem of computational complexity in entity resolution. A poor man's method of entity resolution requires comparison of all entities from the datasets. To reduce the number of comparisons blocking methods have emerged. Meta-blocking is one of the state-of-the-art blocking approaches that introduces new ways to reduce the number of comparisons. The article contribution is to enhance the efficiency of meta-blocking in terms of  Pair Completeness, Pair Quality, F-Measure when using datasets with noisy content. Its efficiency is not better than BLAST when the data is not noisy and Its execution time is roughly two times more than BLAST in all the comparisons of Figure 3.  It produced a blocking with 34% less comparisons than BLAST.

Review 5 (by anonymous reviewer)

(RELEVANCE TO ESWC) Entity resolution of central importance to Linked Data.
(NOVELTY OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) The approach is a combination of existing algorithms for the most part.
(CORRECTNESS AND COMPLETENESS OF THE PROPOSED SOLUTION) The algorithm seems to work well. Minor details of the formal specification should be corrected.
(EVALUATION OF THE STATE-OF-THE-ART) While the approach is compared to BLAST (which I commend the authors for), it totally ignores the state of the art in unsupervised link discovery, which basically achieves the same goals as the authors' approach but with a significantly better F-measure.
(DEMONSTRATION AND DISCUSSION OF THE PROPERTIES OF THE PROPOSED APPROACH) The authors measure both F-measure and runtime.
(REPRODUCIBILITY AND GENERALITY OF THE EXPERIMENTAL STUDY) The code is available, the datasets used are standard and easy to find.
(OVERALL SCORE) The paper presents a blocking approach aiming to accelerate the entity resolution. The assumption stated in the abstract is that heterogeneous data makes blocking more tedious. I will assume that the authors mean "impair the accuracy of blocking techniques" as blocking approaches are commonly designed to deal with all instances of a particular datatype (string, integer, etc.). Their approach is based on Locality Sensitive Hashing (LSH) and is stated to outperform BLAST. 
The authors state that the use of Web systems is a valuable source of data. Do they mean that the use leads to generation of valuable data? The authors then present the problem they address (entity resolution). Their assumption lies in the distributed generation of data leading a.o. to two common errors (typos and misspellings), which they address with their blocking approach under the constraints of data volume and data variety. The blocking techniques considered herein differ from single-attribute blocking approaches such as those implemented in SILK. The authors' approach blocks whole entities and hence needs to be schema-agnostic. 
The approach presented is divided into 3 steps. The formalization of the schema information extraction seems flawed. In particular, the authors used the same variable k for the size of A_e and the number of attributes in D. This contradicts the "loose schema assumption", where |A_e| < k can hold. The authors compute the similarity of attributes in an extensional way using LSH. The signature is generated based on the LSH hashes and the WNP-based algorithm is used for pruning. Basically, the algorithm combine existing algorithms and ideas to NA-BLOCKER.
The evaluation suggests that the NA-BLOCKER basically exchanges runtime (Figure 3) for F-measures (Figure 2). The F-measure achieved by the approach is surprisingly good on Abt-Buy. However, techniques classified as unsupervised link discovery (Nikolov et al., 2012; Zhu et al., 2016, the list is long)  basically do the same and perform significantly better w.r.t. F-measure of the (see, e.g., performance on Amazon-GoogleProducts by Zhu et al., 2016). Moreover, the noise "injection" approach followed by the authors is minimal. Hence, while the authors address a very interesting and important problem, it seems that their approach basically tackles the same problem as unsupervised link discovery under the umbrella of blocking but achieves an F-measure that is poorer than that of the state of the art, while achieving a runtime that is poorer than the state of the art in blocking. This leads to a major problem: either (1) the authors are trying to solve the runtime problem and regard their work as a component that can be of use for unsupervised link discovery or (2) they try to solve the accuracy problem of ER directly while blocking. If (1), then the state of the art seems more scalable. If (2), then unsupervised link discovery approaches perform better. In both cases, it seems to me as if the approach presented herein still needs to either be improved (case 1) or compared with the state of the art in unsupervised linking (case 2) and shown to be better. If (2), I'd strongly suggest that the authors have a look at the works on unsupervised link discovery (see the survey (Nentwig et al., 2017) to start you off), compare their approach at least with the open-source solutions available out there and resubmit the paper toa major Semantic Web conference. This addition should strengthen the case they make for their approach and shed light unto whether this technique really keeps what it promises. I'm afraid I cannot recommend this paper in its current state.
Points
+ Interesting and relevant problem
+ Reuse of existing techniques
+ Evaluation on relevant datasets
- Lack of innovation
- State of the art in unsupervised link discovery not taken into account
- Performance 
Questions
1) How does your approach differ from existing unsupervised link discovery techniques?
2) How much RAM did you assign to the different approaches while running them?

++ After the rebuttal
I agree with the authors that the blocking task and link discovery task are different. However (and here is the crux of the matter) the authors aim to support efficient entity resolution through their blocking scheme by being more accurate but also less efficient. There are obvious contradictory goals here: (1) You want to be more efficient: Then, please outperform the state of the art. (2) You want to achieve a better accuracy: Then compare with accurate methods which achieve good F-measures and show that are close to their accuracy but significantly faster. I appreciate that the authors have improved upon the accuracy of blocking algorithms. However, if the time overhead is too high, then the approach might not be worth being used because ML approaches (see papers cited above) can achieve the same F-measure in less time by running larger link specifications with the competing approach. Hence, I would strongly suggest that the authors show that their approach can indeed be used by *measuring* how well existing open-source link discovery approaches perform when combined with their approach.


Metareview by Hala Skaf

The submission describes a blocking algorithm for Entity Resolution to deal with noise data. Reviewers agree that the topic is  relevant and of central importance to Semantic Web.  
The major issues identified by the reviewers are in particular: 
- Missing Relevant related works from Semantic Web literature. 
- Lacking comparisons with  existing unsupervised link discovery techniques.
-  Performance issues. 
As a result, the submission does not fulfill the requirements for a ESCW publication in terms of maturity and quality.
